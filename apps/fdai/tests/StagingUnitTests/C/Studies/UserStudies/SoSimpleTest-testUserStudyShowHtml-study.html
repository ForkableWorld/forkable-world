Abstract

Sickness Severity was generally 13% lower than average after 420 milligrams of Vyvanase per 21 days.

This data suggests with a low degree of confidence (p=0.0816, 95% CI -0.747 to -0.271) that Vyvanase has a moderately negative predictive relationship (R=-0.509) with Sickness Severity.

The highest quartile of Sickness Severity measurements were observed following an average 165 milligrams Vyvanase per day.

The lowest quartile of Sickness Severity measurements were observed following an average 242 milligrams of Vyvanase per day.

After an onset delay of 30 minutes, Sickness Severity is typically 6% lower than average over the 21 days following around 242 milligrams Vyvanase.

Objective

The objective of this study is to determine the nature of the relationship (if any) between Vyvanase and Sickness Severity. Additionally, we attempt to determine the Vyvanase values most likely to produce optimal Sickness Severity values.

Design

This study is based on data donated by one participant. Thus, the study design is consistent with an n=1 observational natural experiment.

Data Analysis

Vyvanase Pre-Processing

Vyvanase measurement values below 0 milligrams were assumed erroneous and removed. No maximum allowed measurement value was defined for Vyvanase. It was assumed that any gaps in Vyvanase data were unrecorded 0 milligrams measurement values.

Sickness Severity Pre-Processing

Sickness Severity measurement values below 1 out of 5 were assumed erroneous and removed. Sickness Severity measurement values above 5 out of 5 were assumed erroneous and removed. No missing data filling value was defined for Sickness Severity so any gaps in data were just not analyzed instead of assuming zero values for those times.

Predictive Analytics

It was assumed that 30 minutes would pass before a change in Vyvanase would produce an observable change in Sickness Severity.

It was assumed that Vyvanase could produce an observable change in Sickness Severity for as much as 21 days after the stimulus event.

Vyvanase Data Quantity

There are currently 48 raw measurements with 10 changes spanning 161 days from 2012-09-02 to 2013-02-10.

Sickness Severity Data Quantity

There are currently 55 raw measurements with 24 changes spanning 82 days from 2013-01-12 to 2013-04-04.

Statistical Significance

Using a two-tailed t-test with alpha = 0.05, it was determined that the change in Sickness Severity is statistically significant at 95% confidence interval.

After treatment, a 24.5% decrease (-0.676 out of 5) from the mean baseline 2.76 out of 5 was observed. The relative standard deviation at baseline was 24.3%. The observed change was 1.01 times the standard deviation.

A common rule of thumb considers a change greater than twice the baseline standard deviation on two separate pre-post experiments may be considered significant. This occurrence would may have only a 5% likelihood of resulting from random fluctuation (a p-value < 0.05).

Limitations

As with any human experiment, it was impossible to control for all potentially confounding variables. UserVariableRelationship does not necessarily imply causation. We can never know for sure if one factor is definitely the cause of an outcome. However, lack of correlation definitely implies the lack of a causal relationship. Hence, we can with great confidence rule out non-existent relationships. For instance, if we discover no relationship between mood and an antidepressant this information is just as or even more valuable than the discovery that there is a relationship.

We can also take advantage of several characteristics of time series data from many subjects to infer the likelihood of a causal relationship if we do find a correlational relationship. The criteria for causation are a group of minimal conditions necessary to provide adequate evidence of a causal relationship between an incidence and a possible consequence.

Criteria For Causal Inference

Strength (A.K.A. Effect Size)

A small association does not mean that there is not a causal effect, though the larger the association, the more likely that it is causal. There is a moderately negative (R = -0.509) relationship between Vyvanase Intake and Sickness Severity.

Consistency (A.K.A. Reproducibility)

Consistent findings observed by different persons in different places with different samples strengthens the likelihood of an effect. Furthermore, in accordance with the law of large numbers (LLN), the predictive power and accuracy of these results will continually grow over time. 28 paired data points were used in this analysis. Assuming that the relationship is merely coincidental, as the participant independently modifies their Vyvanase Intake values, the observed strength of the relationship will decline until it is below the threshold of significance. To it another way, in the case that we do find a spurious correlation, suggesting that banana intake improves mood for instance, one will likely increase their banana intake. Due to the fact that this correlation is spurious, it is unlikely that you will see a continued and persistent corresponding increase in mood. So over time, the spurious correlation will naturally dissipate.

Specificity

Causation is likely if a very specific population at a specific site and disease with no other likely explanation. The more specific an association between a factor and an effect is, the bigger the probability of a causal relationship.

Temporality

The effect has to occur after the cause (and if there is an expected delay between the cause and expected effect, then the effect must occur after that delay). The confidence in a causal relationship is bolstered by the fact that time-precedence was taken into account in all calculations.

Biological Gradient

Greater exposure should generally lead to greater incidence of the effect. However, in some cases, the mere presence of the factor can trigger the effect. In other cases, an inverse proportion is observed: greater exposure leads to lower incidence.
